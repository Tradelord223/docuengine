# Research Brief: The Metallurgical Crucible

## Core Argument

The documentary should argue that twentieth-century conflict repeatedly converted material limits into strategic problems. The strongest story is not "war creates gadgets." It is "war exposes the exact physical boundary that must be crossed, then forces labs and factories to cross it."

## Verified Anchors

- Science Museum Group frames stainless steel as first cast by Harry Brearley in August 1913 and explains the chromium-based protective film.
- Sir Frank Whittle source material identifies materials as a major objection to early turbojet propulsion and credits Leonard Pfiel/Inco with Nimonic 80 for the W.2B context.
- The National Museum of the U.S. Air Force records the Jumo 004 as the engine of the Me 262 and notes more than 5,000 engines produced by May 1945.
- DOE identifies the Chicago Metallurgical Laboratory as a Manhattan Project branch for plutonium production methods, extraction, and weapon design.
- NPS explains the scale-up from CP-1 to X-10 and Hanford plutonium production.
- Martz, Freibert, and Clark describe wartime plutonium metallurgy and gallium delta-phase stabilization.
- Smithsonian and CIA sources support the A-12/SR-71 titanium and Mach 3 thermal narrative.

## Tone

High-quality, original, sober, and technically literate. The film should feel like material science noir: furnaces, dust, classified rooms, gleaming surfaces, and machines built at the edge of heat.
